Dithering on transitions
Bayer = classic, regular grid pattern (2x2 coarser grain, 8x8 finer). Checkerboard = hard checker pattern. Blue Noise = organic, irregular distribution with no visible pattern — often looks best.
Higher value = dithering covers a wider range of in-between shades. Flat, uniform areas always get the nearest color with no dithering.
Disables dithering in very dark and very bright areas — pixel art usually wants a single clean color there, not "grain".
Detects sharp edges (outlines, small details) and blocks dithering on them, so thin lines and details don't blur into "noise". Lower value = detects more edges.
Outline
Alpha
Pixels with alpha below the threshold become fully transparent (prevents edge "mushiness" after scaling).
Relaxation matching (experimental)
Instead of matching each pixel independently, scores it against 4 criteria at once: color (40%), lightness (30%), agreement with neighbors (20%), shading gradient direction (10%) — over several rounds. Produces more coherent blobs and less random color "flickering", at the cost of processing time. Dithering still runs on top of this result.
Noise / speckle reduction
Fixes semi-transparent edge pixels (a blend of the foreground color and the background from the original PNG) that pick up a stray, "dirty" color from the palette after conversion — a common cause of dark speckles/halos along edges.
A stricter version of the above — only cleans up single pixels that differ from all 8 neighbors. Works independently of "islands" below, you can enable both at once.
Removes small clusters of the same color detached from the rest of the shape (not just single pixels) — if they border a different color, repaints them to match it; if they're "floating" in transparency, simply erases them.
Fills small transparent "holes" inside the silhouette (not touching the image border) with the surrounding color — a common artifact after conversion, e.g. stray see-through spots in shading.
